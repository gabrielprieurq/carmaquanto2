Abstract: This chapter is devoted to quasi maximum likelihood estimation (QMLE) of
the CARMA parameters and estimation of the L√©vy increments of the driving process.
Inference based on irregularly sampled data is dealt with in Section 19.1, but subsequent
sections are concerned with inference based on observations made at regular intervals.
An important aspect of this is the relation between the parameters of the CARMA pro-
cess and the ARMA sequence obtained by sampling it at regular intervals. The sampling
problem was dealt with by Theorem 18.33. In this chapter, we need a corresponding
result for embedding an ARMA sequence into a CARMA process with matching auto-
correlation structure at discrete lags. This is dealt with by Theorem 19.5. An alternative
approach to estimation of CARMA parameters is via Whittle estimation, an excellent ac-
count of which is given by FasenвАУHartmann and Mayer [43]. However, we shall restrict
attention here to QMLE (or its large-sample equivalent, least squares estimation). Illus-
trative examples are given with R programs, which can be copied and pasted into R,
after пђБrst loading the package yuima (Brouste et al. [31]), to reproduce the results. Addi-
tional R programs to generate a variety of L√©vy increments are included in Appendix A
at the end of the book.

Parameter estimation for discrete-time ARMA sequences is usually based on maximiza-
tion of the Gaussian likelihood, i. e., the likelihood of the observations under the assump-
tion that the ARMA sequence is driven by a Gaussian white noise sequence. This estima-
tion procedure is frequently referred to as quasi maximum likelihood estimation (QML
estimation, or QMLE) and can be shown to have certain optimality properties (see Brock-
well and Davis [22]), even when the underlying ARMA sequence is not Gaussian. We shall
adopt the same procedure for CARMA processes.
     When doing maximum likelihood estimation, we shall assume that all zeros of
both the autoregressive polynomial a(z) and the moving average polynomial b(z) of
the CARMA(p, q) process Y lie in the open left half-plane (causality condition and in-
vertibility condition). We shall further assume then that the autoregressive polynomial
a(z) and the moving average polynomial b(z) have real-valued coeпђГcients, and that the
driving L√©vy process L has mean m and пђБnite non-zero variance ќљ2 .
     To compute the Gaussian likelihood we shall assume, whether it is true or not, that
the driving L√©vy process L is Gaussian with mean оАМ(L1 ) = m and Var(L1 ) = ќљ2 вИИ (0, вИЮ)
and determine the likelihood as a function of the coeпђГcients of a causal CARMA(p, q)
model and the parameters m and ќљ2 . The goal of QML estimation is then to maximize
this function with respect to its p + q + 2 arguments.

19.2 QML estimation for regularly sampled CARMA processes
In Section 19.1, we showed how the reduced Gaussian likelihood вДУ(a, b, m) deпђБned in
(19.5) and based on the observed values y1 , . . . , yn of Yt1 , . . . , Ytn can be computed, inde-
pendently of the driving L√©vy process, using the Kalman recursions. The QML estimators
of the parameters a, b and m are values that minimize вДУ(a, b, m). They are not unique,
since to any causal invertible set of minimizing parameters there is a number of non-
causal and/or non-invertible models with the same reduced likelihood. For this reason,
we constrain the minimization of reduced likelihood to causal invertible models.
