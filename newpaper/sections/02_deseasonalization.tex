\section{Data and Deseasonalization}\label{sec:deseasonalization}

\subsection{Electricity Prices}\label{ssec:price-deseasonalization}

\paragraph{Data.}
We use hourly German day-ahead electricity prices for the DE--LU bidding zone.
The calibration sample runs from 1 January 2023 to 31 December 2025 and is
indexed in UTC. The 2021--2025 history is shown in
\Cref{fig:price-window-2021-2025}. The 2021--2022 period contains exceptional
crisis spikes, while the 2023--2025 window keeps a recent regime with both
positive and negative price spikes. Since the German spot market can contain
negative prices, the price is first shifted before applying the logarithm:
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde S_t = \log(S_t + \delta),
  \qquad
  \delta = 1000 \ \text{EUR/MWh}.
  \label{eq:price-log-shift}
\end{equation}
The shift is only used to define a positive log-price factor. Physical prices
are recovered by the inverse map \(S_t = \exp(\widetilde S_t)-\delta\).

\paragraph{Deseasonalization.}
The price model is specified in the shifted log-price coordinate:
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde S_t = \Lambda^P_t + X^P_t .
  \label{eq:price-additive-decomposition}
\end{equation}
Here \(\Lambda^P_t\) is deterministic and \(X^P_t\) is the stochastic price
factor later modeled by CARMA dynamics. We estimate \(\Lambda^P_t\) with a
Paraschiv-style seasonal weight in the transformed coordinate. Let \(\bar p\)
be the sample mean of \(\widetilde S_t\), and let \(d\) be the calendar day
containing hour \(t\). The fitted seasonal component is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\Lambda^P_t
  =
  \bar p\, \widehat F^{Y}_{d}\,\widehat F^{D}_{t},
  \qquad
  \widehat X^P_t = \widetilde S_t - \widehat\Lambda^P_t .
  \label{eq:price-seasonality}
\end{equation}

The day-level factor \(\widehat F^Y_d\) is estimated from daily average
log-prices. If \(\bar S_d^{\log}\) is the daily mean of \(\widetilde S_t\), the
observed factor is
\begin{equation}
  F^{Y,\mathrm{obs}}_d
  =
  \frac{\bar S_d^{\log}}
       {\text{yearly mean of } \bar S_d^{\log}} .
  \label{eq:price-f2y-observed}
\end{equation}
We regress \(F^{Y,\mathrm{obs}}_d\) on weekday and month indicators, with August
split into early and late subperiods. The fitted daily values are broadcast to
the hourly grid.

The intraday factor is estimated on 20 profile classes. Weekdays are grouped by
month, while Saturdays and Sundays are grouped by season. Within a profile class
\(c\), the shifted log-price is regressed on hour-of-day indicators:
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde S_t
  =
  a_0^{(c)}
  +
  \sum_{h=1}^{23} a_h^{(c)}
  \mathbf 1_{\{\operatorname{hour}(t)=h\}}
  +
  \varepsilon_t^{(c)},
  \qquad t \in c .
  \label{eq:price-f2d-regression}
\end{equation}
The fitted intraday curve is normalized by its class mean, which gives
\(\widehat F^D_t\). This normalization makes \(\widehat F^D_t\) a shape factor
rather than a level factor.

This construction removes the main calendar, monthly, and hourly price patterns
before the CARMA calibration. The remaining series \(\widehat X^P\) is treated
as the deseasonalized stochastic log-price factor.

\subsection{Temperature}\label{ssec:temperature-deseasonalization}

\paragraph{Data.}
We use hourly 2-meter temperature for Berlin over the same 2023--2025 UTC
sample. The temperature factor is modeled in physical units, without a
positivity transform:
\begin{equation}
  T_t = \Lambda^T_t + X^T_t ,
  \label{eq:temperature-additive-decomposition}
\end{equation}
where \(\Lambda^T_t\) is deterministic seasonality and \(X^T_t\) is the
deseasonalized stochastic temperature factor.

\paragraph{Deseasonalization.}
The deterministic component is estimated by a Fourier regression with a linear
trend, a daily cycle, an annual cycle, and interactions between the two cycles.
Let \(\tau_t\) be the number of hours elapsed since the beginning of the sample
and set \(P_y=365.25\times24\). The seasonal component is
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
  \Lambda^T_t(\theta)
  =
  \beta_0+\beta_1\tau_t
  &+
  \sum_{k=1}^{3}
  \left[
    a_k\cos\left(\frac{2\pi k\tau_t}{24}\right)
    +
    b_k\sin\left(\frac{2\pi k\tau_t}{24}\right)
  \right] \\
  &+
  a_y\cos\left(\frac{2\pi\tau_t}{P_y}\right)
  +
  b_y\sin\left(\frac{2\pi\tau_t}{P_y}\right) \\
  &+
  \sum_{k=1}^{3}
  \left[
    u_k\cos\left(\frac{2\pi k\tau_t}{24}\right)
    +
    v_k\sin\left(\frac{2\pi k\tau_t}{24}\right)
  \right]\cos\left(\frac{2\pi\tau_t}{P_y}\right) \\
  &+
  \sum_{k=1}^{3}
  \left[
    \widetilde u_k\cos\left(\frac{2\pi k\tau_t}{24}\right)
    +
    \widetilde v_k\sin\left(\frac{2\pi k\tau_t}{24}\right)
  \right]\sin\left(\frac{2\pi\tau_t}{P_y}\right).
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:temperature-seasonality}
\end{equation}
The first sum captures the deterministic intraday pattern, the annual sine and
cosine capture the low-frequency seasonal level, and the last two sums allow the
intraday profile to vary smoothly over the year. All coefficients are estimated
by ordinary least squares:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\theta
  =
  \arg\min_\theta
  \sum_t \bigl(T_t-\Lambda^T_t(\theta)\bigr)^2,
  \qquad
  \widehat X^T_t = T_t-\Lambda^T_t(\widehat\theta).
  \label{eq:temperature-ols}
\end{equation}

\Cref{fig:temperature-seasonal-fit-distribution} shows the fitted deterministic
component over the same week used for the price diagnostics and compares the
marginal distributions of observed and seasonal temperature. The ACF comparison
in \Cref{fig:temperature-acf-before-after} shows that the deterministic
regression removes the dominant periodic component, while the residual retains
serial dependence to be modeled by the temperature CARMA process.

\subsection{Solar Capacity Factors}\label{ssec:solar-deseasonalization}

\paragraph{Data and clear-sky proxy.}
We use hourly German solar capacity factors \(Q_t\in[0,1]\). Solar production
has structural zeros at night and a daylight window that changes over the year,
as shown in \Cref{fig:solar-raw-three-days}. These features make a direct
additive regression on \(Q_t\) inappropriate, since the seasonal level and the
support of the process vary jointly over the calendar.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.97\textwidth]{deseasonalization/solar_raw_three_days.pdf}
  \caption{Raw hourly German solar capacity factor over three days in June 2025.
  The series combines deterministic daylight availability, cloud-driven
  reductions during daylight hours, and structural nighttime zeros.}
  \label{fig:solar-raw-three-days}
\end{figure}

We therefore first estimate an empirical clear-sky proxy. Let \(d(t)\) denote
the day of year and \(h(t)\) the hour of day. For each calendar cell \((d,h)\),
the raw clear-sky level is defined as the upper-tail climatology
\begin{equation}
  C^{\mathrm{raw}}_{d,h}
  =
  q_{0.98}\{Q_t:\ d(t)=d,\ h(t)=h\},
  \label{eq:solar-raw-clearsky}
\end{equation}
where \(q_{0.98}\) is the empirical 98\% quantile. Missing cells are filled by
coarser calendar averages, and each hourly day-of-year profile is smoothed with
a circular 21-day moving average. The resulting proxy is denoted
\begin{equation}
  \widehat C_t = \widehat C_{d(t),h(t)} .
  \label{eq:solar-clearsky-proxy}
\end{equation}
This proxy is an empirical capacity-factor envelope. It captures the seasonal
and intraday availability of solar radiation while keeping the subsequent
stochastic factor bounded.

\paragraph{Latent transformation and deseasonalization.}
The stochastic solar factor is defined from the shortfall of production relative
to the clear-sky proxy:
\begin{equation}
  R_t
  =
  \operatorname{clip}_{[0,1]}
  \left(
    1-\frac{Q_t}{\max(\widehat C_t,\varepsilon_C)}
  \right),
  \qquad
  \varepsilon_C=10^{-4}.
  \label{eq:solar-risk-driver}
\end{equation}
The bounded risk driver is then mapped to a real-valued latent coordinate by a
logit transform,
\begin{equation}
  Z_t
  =
  \operatorname{clip}_{[\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon]}
  \left(
    \frac{R_t-\alpha}{\beta}
  \right),
  \qquad
  Y^Q_t = \log\left(\frac{Z_t}{1-Z_t}\right),
  \label{eq:solar-logit-transform}
\end{equation}
with \(\alpha=10^{-4}\), \(\beta=0.9998\), and \(\varepsilon=10^{-4}\) in the
present calibration. Deseasonalization is performed in this latent coordinate:
\begin{equation}
  Y^Q_t = \Lambda^Q_t + X^Q_t .
  \label{eq:solar-additive-decomposition}
\end{equation}

The deterministic latent component is estimated by ordinary least squares. It
combines hour indicators, month indicators, annual Fourier terms, and
hour-by-annual interactions. With \(H_h(t)=\mathbf 1_{\{h(t)=h\}}\),
\(M_m(t)=\mathbf 1_{\{\operatorname{month}(t)=m\}}\), and
\(\phi_t=2\pi d(t)/365.25\), we use
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
  \Lambda^Q_t(\theta)
  =
  \gamma_0
  &+
  \sum_{h=1}^{23}\gamma_h H_h(t)
  +
  \sum_{m=2}^{12}\eta_m M_m(t) \\
  &+
  \sum_{k=1}^{3}
  \left[
    a_k\cos(k\phi_t)
    +
    b_k\sin(k\phi_t)
  \right] \\
  &+
  \sum_{h=1}^{23} H_h(t)
  \sum_{k=1}^{2}
  \left[
    u_{h,k}\cos(k\phi_t)
    +
    v_{h,k}\sin(k\phi_t)
  \right].
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:solar-seasonality}
\end{equation}
The residual is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\theta
  =
  \arg\min_\theta
  \sum_t \bigl(Y^Q_t-\Lambda^Q_t(\theta)\bigr)^2,
  \qquad
  \widehat X^Q_t=Y^Q_t-\Lambda^Q_t(\widehat\theta).
  \label{eq:solar-ols}
\end{equation}
For visualization in physical units, the fitted latent seasonality is mapped
back to capacity factors through
\begin{equation}
  \widehat Q^{\mathrm{det}}_t
  =
  \widehat C_t
  \left[
    1-\alpha-\beta\sigma\bigl(\Lambda^Q_t(\widehat\theta)\bigr)
  \right],
  \qquad
  \sigma(x)=\frac{1}{1+\exp(-x)} .
  \label{eq:solar-inverse-transform}
\end{equation}

\Cref{fig:solar-transformation-steps} displays the main transformations over
one week. The top panel shows observed production, the clear-sky envelope, and
the latent seasonal component mapped back to capacity-factor scale. The middle
panel shows the bounded shortfall and its normalized version. The bottom panel
shows the latent coordinate \(Y^Q_t\) and its fitted seasonality.

\subsection{Wind Capacity Factors}\label{ssec:wind-deseasonalization}

\paragraph{Data and bounded transform.}
We use hourly German wind capacity factors \(W_t\in[0,1]\), aligned on the
same UTC calendar as the German price panel. Wind does not have structural
nighttime zeros, but the capacity factor is bounded and can spend long periods
near low production levels. \Cref{fig:wind-raw-three-days} shows the raw series
over three days. The model is therefore specified after a logit transformation
of the clipped capacity factor:
\begin{equation}
  W^{\varepsilon}_t
  =
  \operatorname{clip}_{[\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon]}(W_t),
  \qquad
  Y^W_t
  =
  \log\left(\frac{W^{\varepsilon}_t}{1-W^{\varepsilon}_t}\right),
  \qquad
  \varepsilon=10^{-4}.
  \label{eq:wind-logit-transform}
\end{equation}
The clipping only avoids infinite logit values at the boundaries. The inverse
map used for physical diagnostics is
\begin{equation}
  W_t=\sigma(Y^W_t),
  \qquad
  \sigma(x)=\frac{1}{1+\exp(-x)} .
  \label{eq:wind-inverse-logit}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.97\textwidth]{deseasonalization/wind_raw_three_days.pdf}
  \caption{Raw hourly German wind capacity factor over three days in March 2025.
  The process is bounded, persistent, and irregular, without the deterministic
  day-night support constraint present in solar production.}
  \label{fig:wind-raw-three-days}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Deseasonalization.}
Deseasonalization is performed in the logit coordinate:
\begin{equation}
  Y^W_t = \Lambda^W_t + X^W_t .
  \label{eq:wind-additive-decomposition}
\end{equation}
The deterministic component combines intraday, weekly, monthly, and annual
calendar effects. Let \(H_h(t)=\mathbf 1_{\{\operatorname{hour}(t)=h\}}\),
\(D_j(t)=\mathbf 1_{\{\operatorname{weekday}(t)=j\}}\),
\(M_m(t)=\mathbf 1_{\{\operatorname{month}(t)=m\}}\), and
\(\phi_t=2\pi d(t)/365\), where \(d(t)\) is the day of year. The fitted
seasonality is
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
  \Lambda^W_t(\theta)
  =
  \gamma_0
  &+
  \sum_{h=1}^{23}\gamma_h H_h(t)
  +
  \sum_{j=1}^{6}\delta_j D_j(t)
  +
  \sum_{m=2}^{12}\eta_m M_m(t) \\
  &+
  \sum_{k=1}^{2}
  \left[
    a_k\sin(k\phi_t)
    +
    b_k\cos(k\phi_t)
  \right].
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:wind-seasonality}
\end{equation}
All coefficients are estimated by ordinary least squares:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\theta
  =
  \arg\min_\theta
  \sum_t \bigl(Y^W_t-\Lambda^W_t(\theta)\bigr)^2,
  \qquad
  \widehat X^W_t=Y^W_t-\Lambda^W_t(\widehat\theta).
  \label{eq:wind-ols}
\end{equation}
The blue physical-scale curve in the diagnostics is not fitted directly on
\(W_t\). It is the logit seasonality mapped back as
\(\widehat W^{\mathrm{sea}}_t=\sigma(\Lambda^W_t(\widehat\theta))\).

\Cref{fig:wind-seasonal-fit-distribution} gives the one-week physical-scale
view and compares the marginal distributions in the logit coordinate. The ACF
comparison in \Cref{fig:wind-acf-before-after} shows that calendar
deseasonalization removes part of the deterministic periodicity, while the
remaining serial dependence is left for the wind CARMA calibration.
