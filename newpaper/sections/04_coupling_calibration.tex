\section{Coupling Calibration}\label{sec:coupling-calibration}

\subsection{Temperature--Price Coupling}\label{ssec:temperature-price-coupling}

\Cref{fig:temperature-price-raw-residual-scatter} reports the joint cloud of the
deseasonalized temperature residual and the deseasonalized German log-price
residual. The dependence is not visually sharp, but the negative sample
correlation is consistent with the winter heating channel: below-seasonal
temperatures tend to increase electricity demand and spot prices. This relation
is only a reduced-form diagnostic, since the coupled CARMA model acts on shocks
rather than on residual levels.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{coupling/temperature_price_raw_residual_scatter.pdf}
  \caption{Raw residual relation between the deseasonalized German temperature
  residual and the deseasonalized German log-price residual.}
  \label{fig:temperature-price-raw-residual-scatter}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Model.}
The marginal CARMA models from \cref{sec:marginal-carma-calibration} are kept
fixed. Let \(Z^T\) be the temperature state and \(Z^P\) the price state. The
one-way coupled model is
\begin{align}
  dZ_t^T
  &=
  A_T Z_t^T\,dt
  +
  \sigma_T e_T\,dW_t^T,
  &
  Y_t^T &= b_T^\top Z_t^T ,
  \label{eq:temperature-coupling-temperature-state}
  \\
  dZ_t^P
  &=
  A_P Z_t^P\,dt
  +
  \lambda\,\sigma_T e_P\,dW_t^T
  +
  e_P\,dL_t^{P,\mathrm{id}},
  &
  Y_t^P &= b_P^\top Z_t^P .
  \label{eq:temperature-coupling-price-state}
\end{align}
The parameter \(\lambda\) multiplies the common temperature shock in the price
driver. It does not modify the price drift or the deterministic seasonality. A
positive shock in \(Y^T\) is a warmer-than-seasonal temperature shock. Hence a
negative \(\lambda\) means that warmer-than-seasonal shocks lower price shocks,
or equivalently that colder-than-seasonal shocks raise price shocks. This is the
expected winter heating sign, since cold weather increases electric heating
demand. The sign is not structurally constant over the year: in summer,
warmer-than-seasonal shocks can increase cooling demand and therefore raise
prices. The pooled estimate should therefore be read as an average shock
transmission over the calibration window.

\paragraph{Estimator.}
For one-hour intervals, filtered state residuals are computed from the fixed
marginal models:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat R_i^T
  =
  \widehat Z_{t_i+\Delta}^T
  -
  \exp(A_T\Delta)\widehat Z_{t_i}^T,
  \qquad
  \widehat R_i^P
  =
  \widehat Z_{t_i+\Delta}^P
  -
  \exp(A_P\Delta)\widehat Z_{t_i}^P .
  \label{eq:temperature-coupling-state-residuals}
\end{equation}
Under the independence assumption
\(dW^T\perp dL^{P,\mathrm{id}}\), their cross-covariance satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{Cov}(R_i^T,R_i^P)
  =
  \lambda\,\sigma_T^2
  \int_0^\Delta
  \exp(A_Tu)e_T e_P^\top \exp(A_P^\top u)\,du .
  \label{eq:temperature-coupling-covariance-identity}
\end{equation}
With
\[
  \widehat D_{TP}
  =
  \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
  (\widehat R_i^T-\overline R^T)
  (\widehat R_i^P-\overline R^P)^\top
\]
and \(K_{TP}\) denoting the integral in
\cref{eq:temperature-coupling-covariance-identity} for \(\lambda=1\), the scalar
estimate is obtained by projecting through the observable CARMA directions:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\lambda
  =
  \frac{b_T^\top \widehat D_{TP} b_P}
       {b_T^\top K_{TP} b_P}.
  \label{eq:temperature-coupling-lambda-estimator}
\end{equation}
This gives
\[
  \widehat\lambda = -1.3689\times 10^{-3}
\]
on \(26303\) common hourly intervals.

After removing \(\widehat\lambda dL^T\), the linear correlation between the
temperature driver and the idiosyncratic price driver is close to zero, and the
zero-correlation hypothesis is not rejected by the Pearson test. This supports
the independence assumption at the covariance level, while not proving full
distributional independence.

\paragraph{Idiosyncratic driver.}
The recovered marginal price driver contains the historical temperature
component. The driver used for joint simulation is therefore decomposed as
\begin{equation}
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{marg}}
  =
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{id}}
  +
  \widehat\lambda\,dL_i^T,
  \qquad
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{id}}
  =
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{marg}}
  -
  \widehat\lambda\,dL_i^T .
  \label{eq:temperature-coupling-driver-decomposition}
\end{equation}
Both a Gaussian law and a NIG law are fitted to
\(dL^{P,\mathrm{id}}\). The NIG law is retained for simulation.

The transmitted temperature component is much narrower than the idiosyncratic
price driver: the standard deviation of \(\widehat\lambda dL^T\) is
\(5.3811{\times}10^{-4}\), about \(4.4\%\) of the fitted NIG standard
deviation. Thus the coupling is detected in covariance diagnostics, but most of
the marginal price-driver dispersion remains idiosyncratic.

\subsection{Solar--Price Coupling}\label{ssec:solar-price-coupling}

\Cref{fig:solar-price-raw-residual-scatter} reports the same diagnostic for the
solar latent residual. The solar factor is the deseasonalized clear-sky
shortfall variable from \cref{ssec:solar-deseasonalization}, so a positive value
corresponds to a larger latent solar shortfall, not to higher solar production.
The raw residual correlation is positive but small, which is the expected sign
in this transformed coordinate. As for temperature, this is only a level
diagnostic; the coupled model is estimated on the CARMA shocks.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{coupling/solar_price_raw_residual_scatter.pdf}
  \caption{Raw residual relation between the deseasonalized German solar latent
  residual and the deseasonalized German log-price residual.}
  \label{fig:solar-price-raw-residual-scatter}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Model.}
The marginal solar and price CARMA models are kept fixed. Let \(Z^S\) be the
solar latent state and \(Z^P\) the price state. The one-way coupled model is
\begin{align}
  dZ_t^S
  &=
  A_S Z_t^S\,dt
  +
  \sigma_S e_S\,dW_t^S,
  &
  Y_t^S &= b_S^\top Z_t^S ,
  \label{eq:solar-coupling-solar-state}
  \\
  dZ_t^P
  &=
  A_P Z_t^P\,dt
  +
  \lambda\,\sigma_S e_P\,dW_t^S
  +
  e_P\,dL_t^{P,\mathrm{id}},
  &
  Y_t^P &= b_P^\top Z_t^P .
  \label{eq:solar-coupling-price-state}
\end{align}
The parameter \(\lambda\) again acts only through the common shock. The sign must
be interpreted in the transformed coordinate. Since \(Y^S\) is increasing with
latent solar shortfall, a positive \(dW^S\) represents less effective solar
availability, not more production. The merit-order argument therefore predicts a
positive coupling coefficient: larger solar shortfall should increase residual
demand and raise price shocks. Conversely, if the CARMA factor had been defined
directly as above-seasonal production, the expected sign would be negative. The
estimated coefficient below is slightly negative, but its magnitude is
negligible and the driver correlations are statistically indistinguishable from
zero. It is therefore not interpreted as an economic solar-price sign.

\paragraph{Estimator and diagnostics.}
The same covariance projection as in
\cref{eq:temperature-coupling-lambda-estimator} is applied with the solar CARMA
matrices replacing the temperature matrices:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\lambda
  =
  \frac{b_S^\top \widehat D_{SP} b_P}
       {b_S^\top K_{SP} b_P},
  \qquad
  \widehat D_{SP}
  =
  \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
  (\widehat R_i^S-\overline R^S)
  (\widehat R_i^P-\overline R^P)^\top .
  \label{eq:solar-coupling-lambda-estimator}
\end{equation}
This gives
\[
  \widehat\lambda = -7.9685\times 10^{-6}
\]
on \(23103\) common hourly intervals.

The zero-correlation hypothesis is not rejected even before removing the
estimated solar component, and it remains clearly not rejected afterwards. Hence
the solar coupling is negligible at the shock level in this calibration. This
contrasts with temperature, where the raw and marginal-driver correlations are
weak but still statistically detectable before the decomposition.

\paragraph{Idiosyncratic driver.}
The simulation identity is
\begin{equation}
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{marg}}
  =
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{id}}
  +
  \widehat\lambda\,dL_i^S,
  \qquad
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{id}}
  =
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{marg}}
  -
  \widehat\lambda\,dL_i^S .
  \label{eq:solar-coupling-driver-decomposition}
\end{equation}
As for temperature, the NIG law is fitted to the idiosyncratic price driver
rather than to the marginal price driver.

The transmitted solar component has standard deviation

transmission from solar to spot prices.

\subsection{Wind--Price Coupling}\label{ssec:wind-price-coupling}

\Cref{fig:wind-price-raw-residual-scatter} shows the raw relation between the
wind logit residual and the German log-price residual. The wind transform is
monotone in the capacity factor, so a positive wind residual corresponds to
above-seasonal wind production. In contrast with solar, the level relation is
visually and statistically clear: high wind residuals are associated with lower
price residuals. This is consistent with the merit-order effect, since
above-seasonal wind production reduces residual demand and therefore puts
downward pressure on spot prices.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{coupling/wind_price_raw_residual_scatter.pdf}
  \caption{Raw residual relation between the deseasonalized German wind logit
  residual and the deseasonalized German log-price residual.}
  \label{fig:wind-price-raw-residual-scatter}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Model.}
The marginal wind and price CARMA models are kept fixed. Let \(Z^W\) be the wind
state and \(Z^P\) the price state. The one-way coupled model is
\begin{align}
  dZ_t^W
  &=
  A_W Z_t^W\,dt
  +
  \sigma_W e_W\,dW_t^W,
  &
  Y_t^W &= b_W^\top Z_t^W ,
  \label{eq:wind-coupling-wind-state}
  \\
  dZ_t^P
  &=
  A_P Z_t^P\,dt
  +
  \lambda\,\sigma_W e_P\,dW_t^W
  +
  e_P\,dL_t^{P,\mathrm{id}},
  &
  Y_t^P &= b_P^\top Z_t^P .
  \label{eq:wind-coupling-price-state}
\end{align}
The parameter \(\lambda\) is a shock-transmission coefficient. It does not
change the price drift. Because the wind logit transform is increasing in the
physical capacity factor, a positive \(dW^W\) is an above-seasonal wind
production shock. The expected merit-order sign is therefore negative: higher
wind production lowers residual demand and is transmitted as a negative price
shock. This interpretation is direct for wind, unlike solar where the latent
coordinate is a shortfall variable.

\paragraph{Estimator and diagnostics.}
Using the same state-residual covariance projection,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\lambda
  =
  \frac{b_W^\top \widehat D_{WP} b_P}
       {b_W^\top K_{WP} b_P},
  \qquad
  \widehat D_{WP}
  =
  \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
  (\widehat R_i^W-\overline R^W)
  (\widehat R_i^P-\overline R^P)^\top .
  \label{eq:wind-coupling-lambda-estimator}
\end{equation}
This gives
\[
  \widehat\lambda = -1.6913\times 10^{-3}
\]
on \(26304\) common hourly intervals.

The wind channel is therefore identified more clearly than the solar channel.

detectable linear correlation with the wind driver.

\paragraph{Idiosyncratic driver.}
The coupled simulation identity is
\begin{equation}
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{marg}}
  =
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{id}}
  +
  \widehat\lambda\,dL_i^W,
  \qquad
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{id}}
  =
  dL_i^{P,\mathrm{marg}}
  -
  \widehat\lambda\,dL_i^W .
  \label{eq:wind-coupling-driver-decomposition}
\end{equation}
The price driver law used in joint simulation is again fitted after removing the
wind component.

shock-transmission channel to German spot prices.
